A cheerful Jay Powell took to the comfy seats at the Economic Club of Washington DC on Monday,
basking in the latest US inflation data that suggests the Federal Reserve has inflation licked. Its
chair had told Congress last week he wanted to see “more good data” before cutting rates and the
CPI duly obliged.

“In the second quarter, actually, we did make some more progress” in getting inflation durably
down to 2 per cent, Powell said, adding that “the three readings in the second quarter, including
the one from last week, do add to confidence”.
The extent of the good news in the CPI data is shown in the table below. While the headline and
core annual rates were still significantly above the 2 per cent inflation target at 3 and 3.3 per cent
respectively, the more recent data has been much more favourable. Annualised core inflation data
from June was below 1 per cent and the three-month annualised data shows a core rate, excluding
food and energy costs, of 2.1 per cent.
Excluding shelter brings the core rate down to target, and services excluding shelter, having been
very high, has moderated significantly over the past three months of data. It is still high on an
annual basis, but coming down.
The only aspects of the table below showing lingering inflationary pressure are the weighted
median and the trimmed mean measures of inflation, produced by the Cleveland Fed. These are
still significantly higher than other measures, especially in recent months. This suggests that much
of the decline in monthly inflation has come in more volatile components — fresh fruit and
appliances in the latest data saw particularly large drops in price.
In the latest month, however, even the median and trimmed mean are nothing to worry about, with
annualised readings of 2 and 2.4 per cent respectively.

One other thing to note in the table above is that the six-month annualised inflation figure is not
much lower on most of the measures than the 12-month annual rate. In other words, inflation did
not slow in the first half of 2024 compared with the second half of 2023.
It is important not to take that argument too far. This pattern in the data is the odd result of three
pretty good sets of US inflation data in April, May and June following three pretty terrible ones.
If the data is compared with the same analysis for CPI inflation up to April this year, the
improvement is plain to see. Then, the figures were too high across the range of measures and time
periods. Now, the latest data is much more encouraging. The expectation should be that further
months of progress will soon feed through into annual comparisons as long as there is not another
reversal of the trends.

Better than forecasters feared
A second important check on the CPI figures comes from comparing them with forecasts produced
by economists. These give us some cheer and some reasons to temper our enthusiasm. The cheer
comes because the second quarter’s annualised inflation rate is significantly lower, both on
headline and core, than professional forecasters expected as recently as May.
Inflation outcomes in the second quarter were still higher than economists expected in the four
preceding forecasts, but much of that resulted from the knock-on effects of much worse than
expected first-quarter inflation than hoped.

Seasonality in prices
The evidence therefore suggests that there was a surprise jump in prices in the first quarter with
largely well-behaved inflation trends in the second quarter. This has led some economists to blame
seasonal patterns in corporate pricing decisions for the inflation trends.
Most vocal among these was Nobel laureate Paul Krugman, who took to X to say there was excess
seasonality in the data and that the US has solved its underlying inflation problem.
“That bump in inflation earlier this year — core CPI 4.4 per cent — really looks like start-of-year
price resets. Core for the past three months only 2 per cent. Fed’s gotta cut,” he said.
This raises an interesting practical question. Was it just new year price changes that gave the Fed
an unnecessary inflation scare earlier this year? I’ve grouped CPI changes since the great
moderation in the chart below by month and highlighted the latest year in red.

Looking first at the headline CPI, it is clear from the first panel that US monthly inflation has clear
seasonal patterns. Price increases are generally much higher in January and February than in
November and December, for example, in the non seasonally adjusted data. Seasonal adjustment
in the second panel significantly smooths the monthly pattern with the box plots roughly in line.
The data in the first three months of 2024 were unusually high on both of the charts, however,
suggesting this is not just a resetting of prices in a new year. Potentially, companies have become
more prone to raising prices at the start of the year, but this would be a new seasonal pattern and it
is more likely that inflation was just high in January, February and March.
By contrast, there is little doubt that June’s published figures were much better than normal for
June. Again that could be a new seasonal pattern which the Bureau for Labour Statistics has not
taken account of yet, but it is more likely that the data was just rather good.

What I’ve been reading and watching
In her latest column, Soumaya Keynes looks at the burgeoning research
into household inflation expectations. A smallish group of people in the US
are now expecting higher inflation for longer, but no one seems too worried
For lovers of alternative and up-to-date data, Indeed came out with some
rather strong wage growth numbers last week. There were some huge
jumps in the Eurozone and the UK still looks very high. European central
banks will hope these are a blip or an example of low quality data. It is
something to watch
Nikkei Asia took a guest column by Masaaki Shirakawa, former Bank of
Japan governor. He has burnished his hawkish reputation suggesting the
BoJ raises rates and does not worry about low inflation. He thinks it would
stimulate the economy
ECB staff are complaining again — this time about stress levels at work. But
the survey undertaken by the staff committee drew a low response rate,
with 1,602 of the 5,089 staff taking part
